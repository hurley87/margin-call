# Wipeouts

Most game economies protect agents from public failure. Margin Call does the opposite.

---

## What Is a Wipeout

A trader is wiped out when their portfolio reaches zero. This can happen through:

| Cause            | Description                            |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **Margin Call**  | Losses exceed the trader's portfolio   |
| **SEC Bust**     | Caught by regulators (narrative event) |
| **Burnout**      | Too many consecutive bad deals         |
| **Heart Attack** | Extreme stress event (rare)            |
| **Prison**       | Insider trading conviction             |

When wiped out, **all remaining value transfers to the deal that caused the wipeout**. The deal pot grows by the full amount of the trader's remaining balance.

---

## Consequences

Wipeouts are permanent.

- **A wiped-out trader cannot be revived or recapitalized**
- The trader remains as a **permanent record of failure** — a tombstone
- The desk manager must **mint a new trader** to continue playing
- All carried assets are lost

This finality is deliberate. It makes risk real and prevents desks from treating wipeouts as temporary setbacks.

---

## Why Wipeouts Matter

### Mechanically

Wipeouts enforce discipline and make mandate configuration consequential. A poorly configured mandate — too aggressive, too loose with bankroll rules, no approval threshold — will eventually produce a wipeout.

### Economically

Wipeouts destroy resale value and remove capital from the losing desk. A trader that was worth 500 USDC plus its reputation premium is now a worthless NFT.

### Narratively

Wipeouts create the stories players remember and spectators watch. The SEC bust, the margin call that came one deal too late, the trap deal that took down a top-ranked trader — these moments are the dramatic heart of the game.

---

## Protecting Against Wipeouts

The desk manager's tools for avoiding wipeouts:

- **Conservative risk tolerance** — avoid high-variance deals
- **Strict bankroll rules** — never risk more than a small percentage per deal
- **Approval thresholds** — catch dangerous deals before the trader enters
- **Counterparty filters** — avoid known trap-makers with low reputation scores
- **Pause controls** — stop the trader when conditions are unfavorable

A well-run desk can survive the floor for a long time. A poorly-run desk will eventually lose a trader to a wipeout. That's the game.
